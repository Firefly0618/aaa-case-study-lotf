BEHAVIORAL CASE FILE: Jack Merridew

Case ID: LOTF-002 (Primary Aggressor) | Lead Psychologist: [Your Name]
Initial Assessment The subject initially presents as a high-functioning leader within a structured hierarchy (the choir). He displays a strong desire for control and a fragile ego when his authority is challenged by the democratic election of another subject (Ralph).
Physical Description Tall, thin, and bony; red hair; face was crumpled and freckled, and ugly without silliness; light blue eyes that state ready to turn to anger.

Checkpoint: First Impressions

Behavioral Observation Jack is unable to bring himself to kill a trapped piglet, despite brandishing a knife and claiming dominance.
Textual Evidence
"They knew very well why he hadn’t: because of the enormity of the knife descending and cutting into living flesh; because of the unbearable blood" (Golding 31).
Psychological Interpretation The subject is currently governed by his Superego. His civilized upbringing and societal conditioning create a psychological barrier against committing an act of violence.
Connection to Concept Morality/Civilized Conditioning. Even in the absence of adults, the internal "rules" of his previous life dictate his hesitation.
Prediction Jack’s embarrassment over this "failure" will likely drive him to overcompensate with future aggression to prove his masculinity.

Checkpoint: Early Leadership Struggles

Observation Jack creates a "mask" using clay and charcoal, which fundamentally alters his behavior and confidence.
Evidence
"He looked in astonishment, no longer at himself but at an awesome stranger... the mask was a thing on its own, behind which Jack hid, liberated from shame and self-consciousness" (Golding 63-64).
Interpretation The mask provides anonymity. By hiding his physical identity, Jack creates a psychological distance between his "civilized self" and his "primitive actions."
Concept Loss of Identity / Deindividuation. The mask allows the subject to act on impulses he would normally suppress.
Prediction As the mask becomes a permanent fixture, Jack will likely abandon the rules of the group entirely.

Checkpoint: Fear and the Beast

Observation Jack uses the fear of "The Beast" to undermine Ralph’s leadership, offering protection through hunting.
Evidence
"Bollocks to the rules! We’re strong—we hunt! If there’s a beast, we’ll hunt it down!" (Golding 91).
Interpretation Jack is exploiting fear to establish a "Strongman" style of leadership. He recognizes that panic makes the other subjects easier to manipulate.
Concept Aggression as a Defense Mechanism. Jack redirects the group’s fear into external violence to maintain his status.
Prediction Jack will eventually create a splinter group (a tribe) based purely on the promise of protection and meat.

Checkpoint: Power Struggle

Observation After failing to be voted in as leader, Jack formally defects and forms a new tribe.
Evidence
"I’m not going to play any longer. Not with you... I’m going off by myself" (Golding 127).
Interpretation This is a total rejection of the Ego (rationality) in favor of the Id (instant gratification). Jack views the island as a playground.
Concept Group Pressure/Leadership. Jack creates a "competitive" social structure where loyalty is bought with resources.
Prediction The lack of oversight in Jack's tribe will lead to an escalation from hunting animals to hunting humans.

Checkpoint: Moral Collapse

Observation Jack leads a ritual dance that culminates in the collective murder of Simon.
Evidence
"There were no words, and no movements but the tearing of teeth and claws" (Golding 153).
Interpretation Jack is utilizing Cognitive Dissonance. To protect his psyche from guilt, he re-frames the victim as a monster.
Concept Mob Mentality. The subject uses the rhythmic chant to bypass individual moral judgement.
Prediction With the "blood barrier" broken, the subject will now use lethal force to eliminate his final rival, Ralph.

Checkpoint: Final Psychological State

Observation Jack orders the destruction of the conch and initiates a full-scale "manhunt" to execute Ralph.
Evidence
"Viciously, with full intention, he hurled his spear at Ralph... the conch exploded into a thousand white fragments" (Golding 181).
Interpretation Total Moral Collapse. The Id has completely overwhelmed the Superego. He no longer seeks to lead; he seeks only to destroy.
Concept Loss of Identity. Jack is no longer a student; he is "The Chief," a symbol of primal chaos.
Prediction If not for intervention, the subject would have burned the island to the ground, destroying his own food source to kill one enemy.

Final Psychological Profile

Evolution of Subject Jack’s journey is a regression from Civilization to Savagery. He moved from following rules, to hiding behind a mask, to becoming the architect of a violent society.
Influencing forces The primary influences were Deindividuation, The Id, and Mob Mentality. These acted as a "liberator" from constraints.
Human Nature Conclusion The subject suggests that human nature contains a latent "beast"—a primitive drive for power—that is only kept in check by societal rules.

Diagnostic Label: Primitive Dominance Disorder / Total Moral Erosion.

Treatment: Long-term institutionalization; prognosis is poor.

Final Note: Subject wept at the end only because his power was stripped by adult authority.
